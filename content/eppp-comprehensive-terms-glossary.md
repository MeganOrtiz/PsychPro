# PsychPro EPPP Comprehensive Terms Glossary

Purpose: This glossary defines core terms across the eight EPPP Part 1 domains. It is designed to support chapter study guides, search, adaptive review, AI tutor retrieval, and board-style rationale writing. Flashcards should be generated from study content and question performance, not from this terms list alone.

Definition style: concise, clinically useful, and focused on mastery rather than rote memorization.

## Domain I: Biological Bases of Behavior

### Nervous System and Brain Structure

**Central nervous system (CNS):** The brain and spinal cord; responsible for integrating sensory information, coordinating behavior, and regulating cognition, emotion, movement, and autonomic functions.

**Peripheral nervous system (PNS):** The nerves outside the brain and spinal cord; connects the CNS to muscles, organs, and sensory receptors.

**Somatic nervous system:** Division of the PNS involved in voluntary movement and sensory information from the body.

**Autonomic nervous system:** Division of the PNS that regulates involuntary functions such as heart rate, digestion, respiration, and arousal.

**Sympathetic nervous system:** Autonomic branch that prepares the body for threat, stress, or exertion; commonly associated with fight-or-flight responses.

**Parasympathetic nervous system:** Autonomic branch that supports rest, digestion, recovery, and down-regulation of arousal.

**Neuron:** A specialized nerve cell that transmits information through electrical and chemical signaling.

**Synapse:** The junction between neurons where neurotransmitters transmit signals from one neuron to another.

**Neurotransmitter:** A chemical messenger that carries signals across synapses and influences mood, cognition, arousal, reward, movement, and behavior.

**Neuroplasticity:** The nervous system's capacity to change structurally or functionally in response to development, learning, injury, or experience.

**Cerebral cortex:** The outer layer of the brain involved in higher cognitive functions, sensory processing, motor control, language, and conscious awareness.

**Frontal lobe:** Brain region involved in executive functioning, planning, inhibition, personality, motor control, and expressive language.

**Prefrontal cortex:** Frontal region central to judgment, planning, decision-making, working memory, self-regulation, and impulse control.

**Temporal lobe:** Brain region involved in auditory processing, language comprehension, memory, and emotional processing.

**Parietal lobe:** Brain region involved in somatosensory processing, spatial awareness, attention, and integration of sensory information.

**Occipital lobe:** Brain region primarily responsible for visual processing.

**Limbic system:** Interconnected structures involved in emotion, motivation, memory, and threat processing.

**Amygdala:** Limbic structure involved in fear learning, emotional salience, threat detection, and emotional memory.

**Hippocampus:** Brain structure central to new learning, memory consolidation, spatial memory, and contextual memory.

**Hypothalamus:** Brain region involved in homeostasis, endocrine regulation, appetite, temperature, sexual behavior, stress response, and autonomic control.

**Thalamus:** Relay structure that routes sensory and motor information to the cortex, with roles in attention and consciousness.

**Basal ganglia:** Subcortical structures involved in movement, habit learning, reward, and procedural learning.

**Cerebellum:** Brain structure involved in coordination, balance, motor learning, timing, and some cognitive processes.

**Corpus callosum:** Major fiber tract connecting the left and right cerebral hemispheres.

**Reticular activating system:** Brainstem network involved in arousal, alertness, wakefulness, and attention.

### Neurochemistry and Psychopharmacology

**Dopamine:** Neurotransmitter involved in reward, motivation, movement, reinforcement learning, psychosis, and some executive functions.

**Serotonin:** Neurotransmitter involved in mood, anxiety, sleep, appetite, impulsivity, and pain modulation.

**Norepinephrine:** Neurotransmitter involved in arousal, attention, vigilance, stress response, and mood regulation.

**Gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA):** Major inhibitory neurotransmitter; reduces neuronal excitability and is relevant to anxiety, seizures, and sedative effects.

**Glutamate:** Major excitatory neurotransmitter; central to learning, memory, neural plasticity, and excitotoxicity.

**Acetylcholine:** Neurotransmitter involved in attention, memory, learning, muscle activation, and autonomic regulation.

**Endorphins:** Endogenous opioid peptides involved in pain relief, pleasure, and stress response.

**Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI):** Antidepressant class that increases serotonin availability by inhibiting serotonin reuptake.

**Serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (SNRI):** Antidepressant class that increases serotonin and norepinephrine availability.

**Benzodiazepine:** Medication class that enhances GABA activity; used for acute anxiety, insomnia, seizures, and muscle relaxation, with dependence and withdrawal risks.

**Antipsychotic:** Medication class used for psychotic symptoms, mania, agitation, and some augmentation strategies; often affects dopamine and serotonin systems.

**Mood stabilizer:** Medication used to reduce mood episode recurrence or intensity, especially in bipolar disorders.

**Stimulant:** Medication class that increases central nervous system activity, often used for ADHD and narcolepsy.

**Tolerance:** Reduced response to a substance after repeated use, often requiring higher amounts to achieve the same effect.

**Withdrawal:** Physiological and psychological symptoms that occur when substance use is reduced or stopped after dependence develops.

**Intoxication:** Clinically significant behavioral or psychological changes due to recent substance use.

**Akathisia:** Medication-related restlessness and inner agitation, often associated with antipsychotics and sometimes mistaken for anxiety or worsening psychiatric symptoms.

**Tardive dyskinesia:** Potentially persistent involuntary movement disorder associated with long-term dopamine-blocking medication exposure.

**Therapeutic drug monitoring:** Measurement of medication blood levels to evaluate safety, adherence, toxicity risk, or therapeutic range.

### Genetics and Biological Assessment

**Genotype:** An individual's genetic makeup.

**Phenotype:** Observable traits or characteristics that result from genetic and environmental influences.

**Heritability:** The proportion of variation in a trait within a population that is attributable to genetic differences.

**Gene-environment interaction:** A process in which genetic vulnerability or protection changes the effect of environmental exposure.

**Gene-environment correlation:** A process in which genetic factors influence the environments a person experiences or selects.

**Epigenetics:** Changes in gene expression that do not alter DNA sequence but may be influenced by environment, development, stress, or experience.

**Polygenic inheritance:** Influence of many genes, each typically contributing a small effect to a trait or disorder.

**Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI):** Structural imaging method that provides detailed images of brain anatomy.

**Computed tomography (CT):** Structural imaging method often used to detect acute bleeding, lesions, or gross abnormalities.

**Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI):** Imaging method that estimates brain activity by measuring blood-oxygen-level changes.

**Positron emission tomography (PET):** Functional imaging method that uses radioactive tracers to examine metabolism, receptors, or blood flow.

**Electroencephalography (EEG):** Measurement of electrical brain activity, often used in seizure evaluation, sleep studies, and research.

## Domain II: Cognitive-Affective Bases of Behavior

### Intelligence, Cognition, and Information Processing

**General intelligence (g):** A broad cognitive ability factor proposed to underlie performance across diverse intellectual tasks.

**Fluid intelligence:** Capacity to solve novel problems, reason abstractly, and adapt to new situations.

**Crystallized intelligence:** Knowledge and skills acquired through education, culture, language, and experience.

**Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) theory:** Hierarchical model of cognitive abilities that distinguishes broad and narrow abilities, commonly used in modern intelligence test interpretation.

**Working memory:** Capacity to hold and manipulate information briefly for reasoning, learning, and task completion.

**Processing speed:** Efficiency and speed with which a person performs simple cognitive tasks, often affecting academic and functional performance.

**Executive functioning:** Higher-order cognitive processes including inhibition, planning, cognitive flexibility, organization, initiation, monitoring, and problem-solving.

**Cognitive flexibility:** Ability to shift thinking, strategies, or attention in response to changing demands.

**Inhibition:** Ability to suppress automatic, impulsive, or irrelevant responses.

**Attention:** Cognitive process that selects, sustains, divides, or shifts focus toward relevant information.

**Selective attention:** Ability to focus on relevant stimuli while filtering out distractions.

**Sustained attention:** Ability to maintain focus over time.

**Language comprehension:** Ability to understand spoken, written, or signed language.

**Expressive language:** Ability to produce language through speech, writing, or signing.

**Visuospatial processing:** Ability to perceive, analyze, manipulate, and remember spatial relationships.

**Metacognition:** Awareness and regulation of one's own thinking, learning, and problem-solving processes.

### Learning and Memory

**Classical conditioning:** Learning process in which a neutral stimulus becomes associated with an unconditioned stimulus and produces a conditioned response.

**Operant conditioning:** Learning process in which behavior changes based on consequences.

**Reinforcement:** A consequence that increases the future likelihood of a behavior.

**Punishment:** A consequence that decreases the future likelihood of a behavior.

**Positive reinforcement:** Adding something desirable to increase behavior.

**Negative reinforcement:** Removing something aversive to increase behavior.

**Positive punishment:** Adding something aversive to decrease behavior.

**Negative punishment:** Removing something desirable to decrease behavior.

**Extinction:** Decrease in a behavior when reinforcement is discontinued or a conditioned association weakens.

**Extinction burst:** Temporary increase in a behavior after reinforcement is removed.

**Shaping:** Reinforcing successive approximations toward a target behavior.

**Stimulus control:** A situation in which behavior is more likely in the presence of a specific cue or discriminative stimulus.

**Observational learning:** Learning through watching others and the consequences of their behavior.

**Modeling:** Demonstrating a behavior so another person can learn or imitate it.

**Encoding:** Initial processing of information into memory.

**Storage:** Maintenance of information over time.

**Retrieval:** Accessing stored information when needed.

**Episodic memory:** Memory for personally experienced events.

**Semantic memory:** Memory for facts, meanings, and general knowledge.

**Procedural memory:** Memory for skills and habits.

**Prospective memory:** Remembering to perform an intended action in the future.

**Interference:** Memory disruption caused by competing information.

**Retrieval practice:** Learning strategy that strengthens memory through active recall.

**Spaced repetition:** Learning strategy that improves retention by reviewing information across increasing intervals.

### Motivation, Emotion, and Cognition

**Intrinsic motivation:** Motivation driven by interest, enjoyment, meaning, or internal satisfaction.

**Extrinsic motivation:** Motivation driven by external rewards, pressures, or consequences.

**Self-efficacy:** Belief in one's ability to perform a behavior or achieve a goal.

**Self-determination theory:** Motivation theory emphasizing autonomy, competence, and relatedness.

**Expectancy-value theory:** Theory that motivation depends on expected success and perceived value of the goal.

**Emotion regulation:** Processes used to influence emotional experience, expression, intensity, or duration.

**Cognitive appraisal:** Interpretation of an event's meaning, relevance, or threat value.

**Reappraisal:** Emotion regulation strategy that changes emotional response by changing interpretation.

**Suppression:** Emotion regulation strategy that inhibits emotional expression, often without reducing internal emotion.

**Affect:** Observable or subjective emotional tone.

**Mood:** More sustained emotional state that is less tied to a specific trigger than emotion.

**Temperament:** Biologically influenced patterns of emotional reactivity, activity level, and self-regulation.

**Schema:** Organized cognitive framework that influences perception, memory, interpretation, and behavior.

**Automatic thought:** Immediate, often habitual thought that influences emotion and behavior.

**Core belief:** Deeply held belief about self, others, or the world that shapes interpretation and coping.

**Cognitive distortion:** Biased or inaccurate thinking pattern that contributes to distress or maladaptive behavior.

**Avoidance:** Behavior that prevents contact with feared, painful, or demanding stimuli and is often maintained by relief.

## Domain III: Social and Cultural Bases of Behavior

### Social Cognition and Influence

**Social cognition:** How people perceive, interpret, remember, and respond to social information.

**Attribution:** Inference about the cause of behavior or events.

**Fundamental attribution error:** Tendency to overemphasize personal traits and underemphasize situational factors when explaining others' behavior.

**Self-serving bias:** Tendency to attribute successes to internal factors and failures to external factors.

**Confirmation bias:** Tendency to seek, notice, or interpret information in ways that confirm existing beliefs.

**Stereotype:** Cognitive generalization about members of a group.

**Prejudice:** Negative or evaluative attitude toward a group or its members.

**Discrimination:** Behavior that treats people differently based on group membership.

**Implicit bias:** Automatic association or attitude that may influence judgment outside conscious awareness.

**Conformity:** Changing behavior or beliefs to align with group norms or pressure.

**Obedience:** Compliance with commands from an authority figure.

**Persuasion:** Process of changing attitudes or behavior through communication.

**Groupthink:** Poor decision-making that occurs when group cohesion and pressure for agreement suppress critical evaluation.

**Social facilitation:** Improved performance on simple or well-learned tasks in the presence of others.

**Social loafing:** Reduced individual effort when working in a group compared with working alone.

### Relationships, Groups, and Systems

**Attachment:** Emotional bond that shapes expectations about safety, care, closeness, and relationships.

**Empathy:** Capacity to understand or share another person's emotional experience.

**Altruism:** Helping behavior motivated by concern for another person's welfare.

**Aggression:** Behavior intended to harm another person physically, verbally, relationally, or psychologically.

**Reactive aggression:** Impulsive aggression in response to perceived threat, frustration, or provocation.

**Instrumental aggression:** Goal-directed aggression used to obtain a desired outcome.

**Social support:** Emotional, informational, instrumental, or companionship resources provided by others.

**Loneliness:** Subjective distress related to perceived social disconnection or insufficient meaningful connection.

**Family systems theory:** Perspective that family members are interconnected and symptoms may be maintained by interaction patterns.

**Homeostasis:** Tendency of a system to maintain stability, even when existing patterns are problematic.

**Boundary:** Emotional, relational, or role limit that regulates closeness, distance, and responsibility within systems.

**Role:** Expected behavior or function assigned to a person within a group, family, or organization.

**Organizational climate:** Shared perceptions of policies, practices, relationships, and work environment.

**Job satisfaction:** Degree of positive evaluation or contentment with one's work role.

### Culture, Identity, and Oppression

**Culture:** Shared meanings, values, practices, norms, and identities that shape behavior and interpretation.

**Cultural humility:** Ongoing stance of openness, self-reflection, and respect for clients' lived expertise and cultural context.

**Enculturation:** Process of learning and internalizing one's culture of origin.

**Acculturation:** Process of adapting to or interacting with a new cultural context.

**Assimilation:** Process in which a person or group adopts the dominant culture, sometimes with loss of original cultural practices.

**Marginalization:** Exclusion or disconnection from dominant and/or heritage cultural groups and resources.

**Individualism:** Cultural orientation emphasizing independence, autonomy, and individual goals.

**Collectivism:** Cultural orientation emphasizing interdependence, group harmony, and relational obligations.

**Intersectionality:** Framework for understanding how multiple identities interact within systems of power and inequality.

**Minority stress:** Chronic stress experienced by stigmatized groups due to discrimination, rejection, concealment, and structural inequity.

**Microaggression:** Everyday verbal, behavioral, or environmental slight that communicates bias or exclusion.

**Oppression:** Systemic and interpersonal restriction, harm, or inequity based on power differences and group membership.

**Privilege:** Unearned advantage or access associated with social identity or group position.

**Internalized stigma:** Acceptance or absorption of negative societal beliefs about one's own group or condition.

**Stereotype threat:** Performance impairment or stress caused by concern about confirming a negative stereotype.

**Health disparity:** Difference in health outcomes or access linked to social, economic, environmental, or structural disadvantage.

**Explanatory model:** A person's understanding of the cause, meaning, course, and preferred treatment of a problem.

## Domain IV: Growth and Lifespan Development

### Developmental Foundations

**Developmental milestone:** Expected skill or behavior that typically emerges within a broad age range.

**Lifespan development:** Study of physical, cognitive, emotional, and social change from conception through death.

**Sensitive period:** Developmental window during which experience has especially strong influence.

**Critical period:** Limited developmental window during which specific experiences are necessary for typical development.

**Maturation:** Biologically driven developmental change.

**Developmental task:** Age-linked challenge or competency associated with a stage or life period.

**Cohort effect:** Influence of being born and developing during a particular historical period.

**Normative age-graded influence:** Developmental influence commonly linked to age, such as puberty or retirement.

**Normative history-graded influence:** Developmental influence linked to historical events affecting a cohort.

**Nonnormative life event:** Unusual or unexpected event that can alter development, such as early parental death or serious injury.

**Ecological systems theory:** Bronfenbrenner's model describing development within nested environmental systems.

**Microsystem:** Immediate setting in which a person directly participates, such as family, school, or workplace.

**Mesosystem:** Interactions among microsystems, such as family-school relationships.

**Exosystem:** External systems that affect development indirectly, such as parental workplace policies.

**Macrosystem:** Broader cultural, social, economic, and political context.

**Chronosystem:** Influence of time, historical change, and life transitions on development.

### Developmental Theories

**Piaget's cognitive developmental theory:** Stage theory describing changes in children's reasoning from sensorimotor to formal operational thought.

**Sensorimotor stage:** Piaget's first stage, marked by learning through sensory experience and motor action.

**Object permanence:** Understanding that objects continue to exist when not perceived.

**Preoperational stage:** Piaget stage marked by symbolic thought and limitations such as egocentrism and centration.

**Concrete operational stage:** Piaget stage marked by logical reasoning about concrete objects and events.

**Formal operational stage:** Piaget stage marked by abstract, hypothetical, and systematic reasoning.

**Assimilation:** Incorporating new information into existing cognitive schemas.

**Accommodation:** Modifying schemas to incorporate new information.

**Vygotsky's sociocultural theory:** Theory emphasizing social interaction, language, culture, and guided learning in cognitive development.

**Zone of proximal development:** Range between what a learner can do alone and what the learner can do with support.

**Scaffolding:** Temporary support that helps a learner perform a task until independent competence develops.

**Erikson's psychosocial theory:** Lifespan stage theory focused on psychosocial conflicts and identity development.

**Identity versus role confusion:** Erikson's adolescent stage involving exploration and consolidation of identity.

**Generativity versus stagnation:** Erikson's midlife stage involving contribution to others, work, family, or society.

**Kohlberg's moral development theory:** Stage theory describing progression in moral reasoning from obedience to abstract ethical principles.

**Attachment theory:** Theory that early caregiver relationships shape expectations about safety, closeness, and emotion regulation.

**Secure attachment:** Attachment pattern associated with caregiver responsiveness and confidence in caregiver availability.

**Insecure attachment:** Attachment pattern associated with inconsistent, rejecting, frightening, or disrupted caregiving.

### Risk, Resilience, and Family Development

**Risk factor:** Characteristic or condition that increases probability of negative outcome.

**Protective factor:** Characteristic or condition that reduces risk or supports positive adaptation.

**Resilience:** Adaptive functioning or recovery despite adversity.

**Cumulative risk:** Increasing likelihood of negative outcome as multiple risk factors accumulate.

**Differential susceptibility:** Concept that some individuals are more affected by both adverse and supportive environments.

**Family life cycle:** Predictable transitions and developmental tasks families encounter over time.

**Differentiation:** Ability to maintain a clear sense of self while staying emotionally connected to others.

**Role strain:** Stress that occurs when demands of a social or family role exceed resources or conflict with other roles.

**Caregiver burden:** Physical, emotional, financial, or social strain experienced by someone providing care.

**Bereavement:** Experience of loss after death of a significant person.

**Grief:** Emotional, cognitive, behavioral, and physical response to loss.

**Developmental delay:** Slower-than-expected acquisition of skills in one or more developmental domains.

**Developmental regression:** Loss of previously acquired skills.

**Neurodevelopmental disorder:** Disorder with onset in the developmental period that affects cognition, behavior, communication, learning, motor skills, or social functioning.

## Domain V: Assessment and Diagnosis

### Psychometrics and Testing

**Reliability:** Consistency of measurement.

**Validity:** Degree to which evidence supports a specific interpretation and use of test scores.

**Test-retest reliability:** Stability of scores across time.

**Interrater reliability:** Degree of agreement among raters or observers.

**Internal consistency:** Degree to which items on a measure assess the same construct.

**Standardization:** Use of uniform administration, scoring, and interpretation procedures.

**Norms:** Reference data used to compare an individual's score with a defined group.

**Standard score:** Score transformed to indicate position relative to a normative group.

**Percentile rank:** Percentage of the norm group scoring at or below a given score.

**Confidence interval:** Range likely to contain a person's true score within a specified level of confidence.

**Standard error of measurement:** Estimate of score error due to imperfect reliability.

**Sensitivity:** Ability of a test to correctly identify individuals who have a condition.

**Specificity:** Ability of a test to correctly identify individuals who do not have a condition.

**Positive predictive value:** Probability that someone with a positive test result truly has the condition.

**Negative predictive value:** Probability that someone with a negative test result truly does not have the condition.

**Base rate:** Frequency of a condition or characteristic in a population.

**Item difficulty:** Proportion of examinees who answer an item correctly.

**Item discrimination:** Degree to which an item differentiates between higher and lower performers.

**Construct validity:** Evidence that a test measures the intended psychological construct.

**Criterion validity:** Evidence that test scores relate to an external criterion.

**Content validity:** Evidence that test content adequately represents the construct domain.

**Incremental validity:** Degree to which a test adds useful information beyond existing data.

**Test bias:** Systematic error that disadvantages or misrepresents a group.

**Test fairness:** Appropriate and equitable interpretation and use of test scores across individuals and groups.

### Assessment Methods and Diagnostic Reasoning

**Referral question:** The specific clinical, educational, forensic, organizational, or diagnostic question the assessment is intended to answer.

**Clinical interview:** Assessment method using conversation to gather history, symptoms, functioning, context, and client perspective.

**Structured interview:** Interview with standardized questions and scoring to improve reliability.

**Semi-structured interview:** Interview that combines standard questions with clinical flexibility.

**Mental status examination:** Structured observation of appearance, behavior, mood, affect, thought process, thought content, cognition, insight, and judgment.

**Behavioral observation:** Direct observation of behavior in context.

**Collateral information:** Information obtained from other sources, such as family, teachers, records, or providers.

**Self-report measure:** Assessment tool completed by the person being evaluated.

**Informant-report measure:** Assessment tool completed by someone who knows the person being evaluated.

**Adaptive behavior:** Practical, social, and conceptual skills needed for everyday functioning.

**Symptom validity:** Degree to which reported symptoms appear credible and consistent with known patterns.

**Performance validity:** Degree to which test performance reflects genuine ability or effort.

**Differential diagnosis:** Process of distinguishing among possible explanations for symptoms or impairment.

**Comorbidity:** Presence of two or more disorders or conditions in the same person.

**Rule-out:** Diagnostic possibility that requires further evidence before being confirmed or excluded.

**Diagnostic formulation:** Integrated explanation of symptoms, context, development, strengths, risks, and maintaining factors.

**Categorical diagnosis:** Diagnostic approach in which a disorder is considered present or absent based on criteria.

**Dimensional diagnosis:** Diagnostic approach describing symptoms or traits along continua of severity or intensity.

**Clinical significance:** Degree to which a finding or change has meaningful real-world importance.

**Reliable change:** Change that is unlikely to be due to measurement error alone.

### Epidemiology and Decision-Making

**Prevalence:** Proportion of a population with a condition during a defined period.

**Point prevalence:** Proportion of a population with a condition at a specific point in time.

**Lifetime prevalence:** Proportion of a population that has experienced a condition at any point in life.

**Incidence:** Rate of new cases during a defined period.

**Risk ratio:** Comparison of probability of an outcome between two groups.

**Odds ratio:** Comparison of odds of an outcome between two groups.

**False positive:** Test result incorrectly indicating that a condition is present.

**False negative:** Test result incorrectly indicating that a condition is absent.

**Availability heuristic:** Judgment bias in which easily recalled examples seem more common or likely.

**Anchoring bias:** Overreliance on initial information when making judgments.

**Bayesian reasoning:** Updating probability estimates based on prior likelihood and new evidence.

## Domain VI: Treatment, Intervention, Prevention, and Supervision

### Treatment Planning and Intervention

**Evidence-based practice:** Integration of best available research, clinical expertise, and client characteristics, culture, and preferences.

**Treatment plan:** Collaborative plan outlining goals, interventions, responsibilities, and expected outcomes.

**Case conceptualization:** Clinically reasoned explanation of how symptoms developed and are maintained, guiding treatment.

**Treatment matching:** Selecting intervention strategies based on diagnosis, presentation, evidence, client factors, readiness, and context.

**Readiness to change:** Degree to which a person is prepared, willing, and able to make a behavioral or psychological change.

**Stepped care:** Service model that begins with less intensive intervention and increases intensity as needed.

**Therapeutic alliance:** Collaborative emotional bond and agreement on goals and tasks between therapist and client.

**Treatment fidelity:** Degree to which an intervention is delivered as intended.

**Common factors:** Therapeutic elements shared across approaches, such as alliance, expectations, empathy, and collaboration.

**Exposure therapy:** Intervention involving planned contact with feared cues to promote new learning and reduce avoidance.

**Behavioral activation:** Treatment strategy that increases engagement in meaningful or reinforcing activities, often used for depression.

**Cognitive restructuring:** Intervention that helps identify, evaluate, and modify unhelpful thoughts or beliefs.

**Motivational interviewing:** Collaborative, autonomy-supportive approach for resolving ambivalence and strengthening motivation for change.

**Contingency management:** Intervention using systematic reinforcement to increase desired behaviors, often used in substance-use treatment.

**Parent management training:** Behavioral intervention that teaches caregivers to change contingencies affecting child behavior.

**Skills training:** Intervention approach involving instruction, modeling, rehearsal, feedback, and practice.

**Relapse prevention:** Strategies designed to reduce likelihood, severity, or duration of symptom or behavior recurrence.

**Trauma-informed care:** Approach that recognizes trauma effects and emphasizes safety, choice, collaboration, trust, and empowerment.

**Cultural adaptation:** Modification of an intervention to improve relevance, accessibility, and fit while preserving core mechanisms.

### Prevention, Consultation, and Systems

**Primary prevention:** Efforts to prevent problems before they occur.

**Secondary prevention:** Early identification and intervention to reduce severity or duration.

**Tertiary prevention:** Efforts to reduce impairment, relapse, or complications after a condition has developed.

**Universal prevention:** Intervention delivered to an entire population regardless of risk level.

**Selective prevention:** Intervention directed at groups with elevated risk.

**Indicated prevention:** Intervention directed at individuals showing early signs or symptoms.

**Consultation:** Indirect service in which a psychologist helps another person or system address a professional concern.

**Consultee:** Person or group receiving consultation.

**Client system:** Person, group, organization, or setting that is the focus of consultation.

**Needs assessment:** Process of identifying gaps between current conditions and desired conditions.

**Program evaluation:** Systematic assessment of a program's need, implementation, outcomes, or value.

**Integrated care:** Coordinated service model combining behavioral health with medical or other health services.

**Health promotion:** Efforts to support behaviors, environments, and systems that improve well-being.

**Risk reduction:** Efforts to decrease likelihood or impact of harmful outcomes.

**Wellness:** Broad state of physical, psychological, social, occupational, and often spiritual well-being.

### Supervision and Professional Development

**Supervision:** Professional relationship in which a more experienced psychologist oversees, teaches, evaluates, and supports a trainee or less experienced professional.

**Developmental supervision model:** Model that adjusts supervision to the supervisee's level of skill, autonomy, and professional development.

**Competency-based supervision:** Supervision focused on developing and evaluating specific professional competencies.

**Gatekeeping:** Responsibility to monitor and address competence problems to protect clients and the profession.

**Parallel process:** Repetition of relational patterns from therapy within supervision, or from supervision within therapy.

**Corrective feedback:** Specific information about performance that guides improvement.

**Due process in supervision:** Fair procedures for evaluation, remediation, and appeal when supervisee concerns arise.

**Professional competence:** Ability to provide services effectively and ethically within one's training, knowledge, skills, and scope.

**Continuing professional development:** Ongoing learning and skill maintenance across a psychologist's career.

## Domain VII: Research Methods and Statistics

### Research Design

**Independent variable:** Variable manipulated or used to define groups in research.

**Dependent variable:** Outcome variable measured in research.

**Random assignment:** Assignment of participants to conditions by chance to reduce systematic group differences.

**Random sampling:** Selection of participants by chance from a population to improve representativeness.

**Experimental design:** Research design involving manipulation of an independent variable and random assignment.

**Quasi-experimental design:** Design involving comparison or intervention without full random assignment.

**Correlational design:** Research design examining associations among variables without manipulating them.

**Case study:** Intensive study of an individual, group, event, or setting.

**Longitudinal design:** Study design following participants over time.

**Cross-sectional design:** Study design comparing people at one point in time.

**Internal validity:** Degree to which a study supports causal conclusions.

**External validity:** Degree to which findings generalize to other people, settings, times, or conditions.

**Confounding variable:** Extraneous variable that may explain the relationship between variables of interest.

**Selection bias:** Systematic difference between groups due to how participants are selected or assigned.

**Attrition:** Loss of participants over time.

**Demand characteristics:** Cues that influence participants to behave according to perceived study expectations.

### Statistics and Interpretation

**Descriptive statistics:** Statistics that summarize data, such as mean, median, mode, and standard deviation.

**Inferential statistics:** Statistical methods used to draw conclusions about populations from samples.

**Mean:** Arithmetic average of a set of scores.

**Median:** Middle score in an ordered distribution.

**Mode:** Most frequently occurring score.

**Standard deviation:** Average amount scores vary from the mean.

**Normal distribution:** Symmetrical bell-shaped distribution in which most scores cluster near the mean.

**Correlation:** Statistic describing direction and strength of association between variables.

**Regression:** Statistical method used to predict an outcome from one or more predictors.

**Analysis of variance (ANOVA):** Statistical test comparing means across groups.

**t test:** Statistical test comparing means between two groups or conditions.

**Chi-square test:** Statistical test used with categorical data to examine associations or differences.

**p value:** Probability of obtaining results at least as extreme as observed if the null hypothesis were true.

**Null hypothesis:** Statement that there is no effect or no relationship.

**Type I error:** Incorrectly rejecting a true null hypothesis; a false positive.

**Type II error:** Failing to reject a false null hypothesis; a false negative.

**Statistical power:** Probability of detecting an effect when one exists.

**Effect size:** Quantitative estimate of the magnitude of a relationship or difference.

**Confidence interval:** Range of plausible values for a population parameter.

**Meta-analysis:** Statistical synthesis of results across multiple studies.

**Factor analysis:** Statistical method used to identify underlying dimensions or factors among variables.

### Evaluation and Dissemination

**Formative evaluation:** Evaluation used to improve a program during development or implementation.

**Summative evaluation:** Evaluation used to judge the overall merit or outcome of a program.

**Process evaluation:** Evaluation of whether a program is being implemented as intended.

**Outcome evaluation:** Evaluation of program effects or results.

**Cost-benefit analysis:** Comparison of program costs with monetary or practical benefits.

**Community-based participatory research:** Collaborative research approach involving community members as partners in the research process.

**Dissemination:** Communication of research findings to relevant audiences.

**Publication bias:** Tendency for studies with significant or favorable results to be published more often than null or unfavorable findings.

## Domain VIII: Ethical, Legal, and Professional Issues

### Ethical Foundations

**Ethics code:** Formal set of principles and standards guiding professional conduct.

**Beneficence:** Ethical principle of promoting welfare and benefit.

**Nonmaleficence:** Ethical principle of avoiding or minimizing harm.

**Fidelity:** Ethical principle involving trust, responsibility, and honoring professional commitments.

**Integrity:** Ethical principle involving honesty, accuracy, and truthfulness.

**Justice:** Ethical principle involving fairness and equitable access to benefits and protections.

**Respect for rights and dignity:** Ethical principle emphasizing autonomy, privacy, confidentiality, and worth of persons.

**Aspirational principle:** Broad ethical ideal that guides professional behavior.

**Enforceable standard:** Specific ethical rule that can be used in professional discipline.

**Informed consent:** Process of providing understandable information about services, risks, benefits, alternatives, limits, and rights so a person can make a voluntary decision.

**Assent:** Developmentally appropriate agreement to participate, often used with minors or individuals who cannot provide full legal consent.

**Confidentiality:** Ethical duty to protect private information obtained in professional relationships.

**Privilege:** Legal protection that can prevent disclosure of confidential communications in legal proceedings.

**Privacy:** Person's right to control access to personal information and personal space.

**Multiple relationship:** Situation in which a psychologist has more than one role with a person or closely related person.

**Conflict of interest:** Situation in which personal, financial, professional, or institutional interests may impair objectivity or effectiveness.

**Boundary crossing:** Departure from typical professional boundaries that may be harmless, helpful, or risky depending on context.

**Boundary violation:** Harmful or exploitative boundary departure.

### Legal and Professional Responsibilities

**Mandatory reporting:** Legal duty to report specified concerns such as child abuse, elder abuse, vulnerable adult abuse, or certain threats, depending on jurisdiction.

**Duty to protect:** Legal or ethical obligation to take reasonable steps to protect identifiable potential victims or others from serious threat.

**Duty to warn:** Obligation in some jurisdictions to warn identifiable potential victims of serious threat.

**Standard of care:** Level of care expected of a reasonably competent professional under similar circumstances.

**Scope of practice:** Services a psychologist is legally and professionally authorized and competent to provide.

**Scope of competence:** Services a psychologist can ethically provide based on education, training, supervised experience, consultation, and practice.

**Jurisdiction:** Legal authority of a state, province, territory, or court over professional practice or legal matters.

**Recordkeeping:** Creation and maintenance of accurate professional documentation.

**Client access to records:** Client or patient right, subject to law and exceptions, to inspect or obtain records.

**Test security:** Protection of psychological test materials to preserve validity and prevent misuse.

**Forensic evaluation:** Psychological evaluation conducted to answer legal questions rather than provide treatment.

**Mandated client:** Person required by court, employer, school, or agency to participate in services or evaluation.

**Expert testimony:** Professional opinion presented in legal proceedings based on specialized knowledge.

**Impaired psychologist:** Psychologist whose professional functioning is compromised by health, substance use, distress, competence problems, or other factors.

### Ethics in Specialized Contexts

**Ethical decision-making model:** Structured process for identifying ethical issues, gathering facts, consulting standards and laws, weighing options, acting, documenting, and monitoring.

**Consultation in ethics:** Seeking professional guidance to improve ethical reasoning, reduce bias, and manage complexity.

**Research ethics:** Principles and standards protecting participants and scientific integrity.

**Institutional review board (IRB):** Committee that reviews research involving human participants for ethical acceptability.

**Debriefing:** Providing participants with information after research participation, especially when deception was used.

**Undue influence:** Excessive or inappropriate pressure that compromises voluntary consent.

**Coercion:** Use of threat or force that undermines voluntary participation.

**Fabrication:** Making up data or results.

**Falsification:** Manipulating research materials, processes, data, or results.

**Plagiarism:** Presenting another person's words, ideas, or work as one's own.

**Telepsychology:** Psychological services delivered using telecommunication technology.

**Technology-assisted services:** Psychological services supported by digital tools such as video, messaging, apps, portals, remote assessment, or AI systems.

**Digital privacy:** Protection of personal information in electronic systems and communications.

**Social media boundary:** Professional boundary issue involving online interactions, public content, searching, messaging, or dual-role risk.

**Emergency planning:** Preparation for crisis response, location verification, backup contact, and local resources, especially in telepsychology.

**Algorithmic bias:** Systematic unfairness or error in automated systems that disadvantages certain individuals or groups.

## Cross-Domain Integration Terms

**Biopsychosocial model:** Framework explaining behavior and health through biological, psychological, and social influences.

**Case formulation:** Integrated clinical explanation of a person's difficulties, strengths, context, maintaining factors, and intervention targets.

**Clinical reasoning:** Process of using evidence, theory, assessment, ethics, and context to make professional judgments.

**Cultural responsiveness:** Practice of adapting understanding and intervention to cultural identity, context, power, and lived experience.

**Evidence-based learning:** Learning approach grounded in research on memory, retrieval, feedback, spacing, and application.

**Mastery:** Durable ability to understand, apply, distinguish, and teach a concept across contexts.

**Competency mapping:** Linking content, questions, performance, and review tasks to defined knowledge statements or professional competencies.

**Adaptive review:** Review system that prioritizes content based on performance, confidence, recency, difficulty, and learning needs.

**Clinical application:** Use of knowledge to interpret, assess, diagnose, treat, consult, supervise, or make ethical decisions.

**Knowledge integration:** Ability to connect concepts across domains rather than treating them as isolated facts.
