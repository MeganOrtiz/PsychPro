🔑 Major Types of Dyslexia
1. Phonological Dyslexia (Most Common)
Core deficit: Phonological processing (sound structure of language)
🧩 Neurocognitive Profile
Weak phonemic awareness (blending, segmenting)
Difficulty mapping letters → sounds
Impaired decoding of unfamiliar words
Reliance on memorization rather than rules
📚 Reading Pattern
Poor decoding of nonwords (e.g., “lat,” “mip”)
Slow, effortful reading
Frequent phonetic errors (e.g., “ship” → “sip”)
🧠 Neural Correlates
Left temporoparietal dysfunction (phonological network)
Reduced activation in inferior frontal and posterior regions
⚠️ Functional Impact
Early reading delays
Difficulty learning phonics-based instruction
Avoidance of reading-intensive tasks
2. Surface Dyslexia (Orthographic Dyslexia)
Core deficit: Orthographic processing (visual word recognition)
🧩 Neurocognitive Profile
Difficulty storing and retrieving whole-word representations
Weak sight word recognition
Over-reliance on phonological decoding
📚 Reading Pattern
Difficulty with irregular words (e.g., “yacht,” “colonel”)
Regularization errors (e.g., “pint” → pronounced like “mint”)
Slow reading despite intact decoding
🧠 Neural Correlates
Left occipitotemporal region (Visual Word Form Area)
⚠️ Functional Impact
Reduced reading fluency
Struggles with spelling irregular words
Labor-intensive reading even with age
3. Rapid Naming Deficit (RAN Dyslexia)
Core deficit: Processing speed / automaticity of retrieval
🧩 Neurocognitive Profile
Slow rapid automatized naming (letters, numbers, colors)
Inefficient access to phonological representations
Reduced automaticity
📚 Reading Pattern
Slow, labored reading
Difficulty with reading fluency (not accuracy per se)
Trouble keeping pace with peers
🧠 Neural Correlates
Distributed network inefficiency (frontal + occipitotemporal connectivity)
⚠️ Functional Impact
Reading is accurate but not automatic
Fatigue with sustained reading
Impaired comprehension due to slowed rate
4. Double Deficit Dyslexia
Core deficit: Combined phonological + rapid naming deficits
🧩 Neurocognitive Profile
Impairments in both phonological processing AND naming speed
📚 Reading Pattern
Poor decoding + slow fluency
Most severe and persistent presentation
🧠 Clinical Significance
Associated with poorer prognosis
Requires intensive, multi-component intervention
5. Visual (or Visual-Attentional) Dyslexia
Core deficit: Visual attention span / orthographic processing efficiency
🧩 Neurocognitive Profile
Difficulty processing multiple letters simultaneously
Reduced visual attention span
Letter position errors
📚 Reading Pattern
Skipping words/lines
Reversals or transpositions (e.g., “form” → “from”)
Difficulty tracking text
🧠 Neural Correlates
Parietal-occipital regions (visual attention networks)
⚠️ Clinical Note
Less universally accepted as a “core” dyslexia subtype
Often overlaps with attentional/executive weaknesses (e.g., ADHD)
6. Deep Dyslexia (Rare; Acquired > Developmental)
Core deficit: Semantic processing disruption
🧩 Neurocognitive Profile
Breakdown in mapping orthography → semantics
📚 Reading Pattern
Semantic errors (e.g., “dog” → “cat”)
Difficulty reading abstract words
Better performance on concrete words
🧠 Clinical Note
More common in acquired dyslexia (brain injury)
Rare in developmental presentations
