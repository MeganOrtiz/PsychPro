Hearing 
-Sound 
Amplitude- intensity of the sound wave 
Frequency- number of compressions per second 
Pitch 
Timber- tone quality or complexity 
Humans not adapted for pure tones 
Sound waves can be added together to change sound  
-Structures of the ear 
Outer 
Pinna- ear attachment to each side of the head 
External auditory canal- tube extending to ear drum and tympanic membrane 
Locates source of sound 
Middle  
Tympanic membrane- ear drum 
Vibrates when struck by sounds 
Connects to the tiny bones 
Tiny bones- malleus, incus, stapes 
Transform waves into stronger waves to project onto oval window 
Inner 
Oval window- membrane that transmits waves through the viscous fluid in the inner ear 
Cochlea- fluid filled structure  
Vestibular system- 3 fluid filled tubes 
Organ of Corti 
Basilar membrane- auditory receptors and hair cells  
Tectorial membrane- rests on hair cells  
Action potential when hair cells move and create electrical impulse 
-Process 
External sound comes in and strikes the tympanic membrane (ear drum)--> ossicles in middle ear vibrate--> cochlea--> basilar membrane 
-Pathway 
External stimuli coming in--> auditory nerve (VIII)--> cochlear nuclei (pons/medulla)--> superior olives (pons)--> inferior colliculi (midbrain)--> medial geniculate nucleus (MGN) (thalamus)--> primary auditory cortex (temporal lobe) 
-Structure function 
Medial and lateral superior olives  
In the pons 
Medial- arrival time information 
Lateral- amplitude information 
Superior colliculus 
In the midbrain 
Localization of sound 
-Tonotopic map 
Cells are more responsive to preferred or certain tones 
Cells responsive to similar frequencies tend to group together 
Organized by frequency 
Detail differs from person to person 
Respond better to pure tones 
-Secondary and associated cortex 
Response to speech 
-Visual-auditory integration 
Only in the association cortex
