Amino Acid Neurotransmitters 
 
-Glutamate 
EXCITATORY 
RAPID 
Produces fast, postsynaptic responses 
Overall modulation 
Lowers negative charge in the neuron making it more positive  
Precursor to GABA 
90% of receptors in the brain have target sites for glutamate 
Involved in protein synthesis 
Pool of glutamate kept separate for 1)neurotransmission 2)protein synthesis 
Needs to be converted to glutamine from glutamate by glutamine synthetase to be able to return back to the presynaptic neuron 
Drug development 
Movement away from the MAO to treat mood disorders  
Esketamine (Spravato) 
Target the NMDA receptors  
Nasal spray 
Prescribed for treatment resistant depression 
Synthesis and degradation 
Glutamine ---> glutaminase ---> glutamate 
Glutamine ---> glutamine synthetase ---> glutamine (in glia)

Pharmacology 
 
-Conceptual foundation 
A drug is an intruder to the brain 
Want to achieve balance between helping and harming effects 
Exogenous psychotropic medication will heighten or diminish the endogenous functional neurochemistry of the brain, or can have no impact 
-Drugs and the body (time based) 
Pharmacokinetics (PK) 
What the body does to the drug 
Pharmacodynamics (PD) 
What the drug does to the body 
-Pharmacokinetics 
Absorption 
How the drug enters the body 
Includes: 
Route of administration 
Enteral 
Includes the gastrointestinal tract 
Most common 
Drug must be soluble and stable in stomach fluid  
First pass metabolism 
Must pass through the liver  
Enzymes break drugs down 
Oral/rectal 
Parenteral 
Does not involve the gastrointestinal tract, bypass 
Injection (IV/IM/SubQ) 
More prompt response than oral bc faster absorption 
No loss of bioavailability 
Rapid rate of absorption can be problematic if adverse effects are experienced 
Can't use an oily solution 
Inhalation 
Mouth or nose 
Drugs more commonly address respiratory disease than psychiatric disorders  
Transcutaneous 
Transdermal skin patch 
Mucous membranes 
Dose 
Therapeutic range 
Dosage form 
Distribution 
How the drugs finds its way throughout the body 
Includes: 
The blood brain barrier (BBB) 
Active substance can be distributed to many areas of the brain, both those intended and unintended 
Metabolism 
How the drug is broken down 
Includes: 
Biotransformation 
Into a more water – soluble agent to be less biologically active 
Breakdown of the active substance into metabolites 
Half life 
The time for the plasma level f the drug to fall by 50% 
Determines when the drug needs to be taken again 
Most drugs are designed to be more than once 
Need to achieve a steady state 
Therapeutic level of medication needs to be effective  
Elimination 
How the drug leaves the body 
Usually urine 
-Pharmacodynamics 
Concentration of the site of action 
Contribute to desired therapeutic effects 
2 components: 
What is happening in the field of play? 
Pharmacologic effect/mechanism of action 
What is happening clinically? 
Blocking, destroying, etc 
Subjective in nature  
Clinical response 
Side effects 
Bodily structures 
Functional systems 
Specific organs  
Specific CNS regions 
Toxicity 
Change in nature of adverse events  
Ex: change from attentional problems to disorientation to coma 
Change in severity of adverse events  
Ex: change from mild to severe headache  
Many side effects are transient at the onset of a new medication or when the dose in changed on a current medication 
Some can be more enduring that lead to lasting effects such as allergic reactions, etc.  
Generic medication can have other inactive substances that impact the efficacy and side effects of the drug
